Section B — Poetry Tiers (Old 3rd Sem)

TIER 1 — CERTAIN

"Virtue" — George Herbert

"Only a sweet and virtuous soul, / Like seasoned timber, never gives"

Theme: Transience of earthly beauty vs immortality of virtue. Spring dies, rose dies, day dies — but virtue survives. Stanza explanation format expected.

"Belinda's Dressing Table" — Pope

"And now, unveiled, the Toilet stands displayed, / Each silver Vase in mystic order laid"

Theme: Mock-heroic satire on aristocratic vanity. Pope ridicules the rituals of fashionable women by treating them as epic ceremonies. 18th century England target.

TIER 2

"Ulysses" — Tennyson

"To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield"

Spirit of adventure. Dramatic monologue. Old king refuses to accept a sedentary life. "It little profits that an idle king..."

"To a Skylark" — Shelley

"Hail to thee, blithe Spirit! / Bird thou never wert"

Skylark = ideal poet, pure joy without pain. Compared to maiden, glow-worm, rose. "Our sweetest songs are those that tell of saddest thought."

Sonnet 73 — Shakespeare

"That time of year thou mayst in me behold / When yellow leaves, or none, or few, do hang"

Three metaphors: autumn branches, twilight, dying fire. All convey aging. Love intensified by awareness of approaching death.

TIER 3

"La Belle Dame Sans Merci" — Keats. Fatal attraction of a fairy woman who destroys a knight. "And this is why I sojourn here, / Alone and palely loitering." Know the plot: knight meets lady → dream of pale kings → wakes alone. Pallor and coldness throughout.

Section C — Poetry Tiers (Old 4th Sem — HIGH CERTAINTY)

TIER 1 — NEAR CERTAIN (Both will appear)

"The Emperor of Ice Cream" — Stevens

"Let be be finale of seem. / The only emperor is the emperor of ice-cream."

100% hit rate, 7/7 years. Theme: the reality of death over appearance. Two stanzas — kitchen (life/pleasure) and bedroom (death/body). "Let be be finale of seem" = accept reality. Ice cream = perishable, earthly pleasure.

Magic words: reality vs appearance, carpe diem, mortality, hedonism, the mundane

"Ah, Are You Digging on My Grave?" — Hardy

"Then who is digging on my grave? / Say — since I have not guessed!"

86% hit rate. Dark irony — dead woman asks who digs her grave; turns out it's just her dog burying a bone. Reveals futility: lover moved on, kin forgot, foe indifferent, dog has no loyalty. Hardy's pessimism about human relationships.

Magic words: dark irony, pessimism, futility, indifference after death, human forgetfulness

TIER 1 — VERY HIGH

"The Wild Swans at Coole" — W.B. Yeats

"All's changed since I, hearing at twilight, / The first time on this shore"

71% hit rate. Theme: passage of time, the immortality of natural beauty contrasted with human aging. Yeats first saw the swans 19 years ago; they are still there, unchanged. 59 swans rise in pairs — symbol of undying passion and mystery. The poet feels old and weary while nature endures.

Magic words: transience, passage of time, beauty, passion, permanence of nature vs human aging

TIER 2 — HIGH & RISING

"Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening" — Robert Frost

"The woods are lovely, dark and deep, / But I have promises to keep"

Appeared 3 consecutive years (2023, 2024, 2025). Rising trend — will almost certainly appear. Rider pauses at dark snowy woods, tempted to stay. "Promises to keep" = duty over temptation. Dark woods = symbolic death/rest. Simple language, deep theme.

Phase 4 — 80/20 Study Plan (What Exactly to Study)

SECTION A — Grammar (20% of your time)

You are already strong here. Just warm-up revision. Focus only on these exact task types:

  • Can / could / be able to: "Gary has travelled a lot. He _____ speak five languages." → can
  • Must / can't: "You've been travelling all day. You _____ be tired." → must
  • Get + past participle: "There was a fight. Nobody _____ (hurt)." → got hurt
  • Word order: Practice putting scrambled sentences with "had" and auxiliary verbs in order — e.g., "had/in the kitchen/fitted/some new cupboards/we've"
  • Relative clauses: "A bus goes to the airport. It runs every half hour." → "A bus that goes to the airport runs every half hour."
  • Unless / as long as: "I'm playing tennis tomorrow unless it rains."
  • Although / because of: "I went home early because I was feeling unwell."
  • Should/shouldn't: "The speed limit is 30. Kate is doing 50. She shouldn't be driving so fast."
  • Passive: "Five hundred people _____ (employ) there." → are employed
Grammar tip: Q1 typically has 10 sub-parts (1 mark each = 10 marks). Q2 has 10 sub-parts. You need to attempt 2 questions from Section A but there are only 2 questions SET. This means you MUST attempt both A questions. Know all the above patterns cold.

SECTION B — Poems 1–6 (25% of your time)

Must attempt 1 question. There will be 2 questions — choose the poem you know best. Focus on these specific talking points:

Virtue (George Herbert) — MASTER THIS

  • Theme: Everything earthly dies (spring, rose, day) but virtue survives death
  • Structure: 4 stanzas — each stanza praises earthly beauty then says "it must die"
  • Final twist: "Only a sweet and virtuous soul, / Like seasoned timber, never gives" — virtue = timber (survives fire)
  • Key stanza to explain: "Sweet spring, full of sweet days and roses / A box where sweets compacted lie / My music shows ye have your closes / And all must die"
  • Literary devices: anaphora (repetition of "Sweet day"), paradox, extended metaphor
  • Examiner's magic words: transience, immortality, Christian virtue, conceit, carpe diem vs stoicism

Belinda's Dressing Table (Pope) — MASTER THIS

  • From: The Rape of the Lock (mock-epic / mock-heroic poem)
  • Pope mocks 18th century aristocratic women by describing dressing table rituals in epic/heroic language
  • The dressing table = an "altar" → Belinda = a vain goddess
  • Key argument: Pope is indeed making fun of Belinda and fashionable society (exam question: "Is Pope making fun of Belinda?") → Answer: Yes — by elevating trivial acts to epic scale, he exposes their absurdity
  • Literary devices: mock-epic, hyperbole, irony, allusion
  • Examiner's magic words: satire, mock-heroic, vanity, 18th century society, irony, heroic couplets

Quick notes for Q backup choices

  • Ulysses: "To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield" — theme: refusing old age. "It little profits that an idle king..." opens the poem. Trace thought: boredom → adventure call → recruiting crew → resolution
  • To a Skylark: "Hail to thee, blithe Spirit! / Bird thou never wert" — not a real bird but pure inspiration. Compared to: maiden, glow-worm, rose, poet, high-born maiden. "Our sweetest songs are those that tell of saddest thought" — human poetry = sadness, bird's song = pure joy
  • Sonnet 73: 3 metaphors = autumn boughs (old age) + twilight/sunset (end of day = end of life) + dying embers (love fading). Couplet: "This thou perceiv'st, which makes thy love more strong / To love that well which thou must leave ere long" — love intensified by impermanence

SECTION C — Poems 7–12 (35% of your time — MAXIMUM INVESTMENT)

This is your mark-securing section. Hardy and Stevens appear EVERY year. Must attempt 1 question — 2 will be set.

"The Emperor of Ice Cream" — Wallace Stevens [100% HIT RATE]

  • Title interpretation: "Emperor of Ice Cream" = the emperor of reality/transience. Ice cream melts — symbol of earthly pleasure that cannot last
  • Stanza 1 (kitchen scene): life, pleasure, wench, cigars, boys — mundane pleasures of the living
  • Stanza 2 (bedroom scene): dead woman's body, her embroidered sheet with birds failing to conceal her cold feet → reality of death exposed
  • Key line: "Let be be finale of seem" → accept what IS, not what APPEARS. Reality over illusion
  • Second key line: "The only emperor is the emperor of ice-cream" = repeated twice, emphasizing the acceptance of life's perishable nature
  • Examiner's magic words: acceptance of mortality, reality vs appearance, Modernism, the mundane, anti-romanticism, carpe diem
  • Question types seen: "What is the central idea?" / "Comment on the title" / "What is the theme?"

"Ah, Are You Digging on My Grave?" — Thomas Hardy [86% HIT RATE]

  • Structure: dramatic dialogue — dead woman asks who digs her grave through 5 stanzas
  • Stanza by stanza: lover? (no, he remarried) → kin? (no, they planted a tree) → foe? (no, indifferent) → finally: her dog! But the dog was just burying a bone, not visiting out of loyalty
  • Theme: Hardy's bleak view — even love, hate, and loyalty fade after death. Futility. Indifference of the living to the dead. Dark humor through irony
  • Key stanza to quote: "Then, who is digging on my grave? / Say — since I have not guessed!" and the dog's reply: "O it is I, my mistress dear, / Your little dog, who still lives near"
  • Examiner's magic words: dark irony, pessimism, futility, indifference, human relationships post-death, dramatic irony, Hardy's worldview
  • Question style: "What does this poem tell you about Hardy's view of human life and relationships?"

"The Wild Swans at Coole" — W.B. Yeats [71% HIT RATE]

  • Setting: October 1916, twilight, 59 swans on the lake. Yeats has visited 19 years before
  • Central emotion: sadness. The swans are unchanged — still paired, still passionate. The poet has aged, lost passion, changed
  • Key stanza: "All's changed since I, hearing at twilight, / The first time on this shore, / The bell-beat of their wings above my head, / Trod with a lighter tread"
  • Swans = symbol of eternal beauty, passion, youth, mystery. Poet = human aging and loss
  • Final question in poem: "Among what rushes will they build... / ...And what man's or woman's heart will they delight?" — Yeats knows the swans will outlast him
  • Examiner's magic words: transience of human life, permanence of natural beauty, nostalgia, Celtic Twilight, Yeats's personal melancholy, symbolism

"Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening" — Frost [RISING TREND]

  • Setting: Winter evening, rider stops to watch snow fill the woods. Owner of woods lives in village
  • Contrast: beautiful, dark, peaceful woods vs duty and "promises to keep"
  • Key stanza: "The woods are lovely, dark and deep / But I have promises to keep / And miles to go before I sleep" (repeated — emphasis on duty)
  • Symbolic reading: dark woods = temptation of death/rest/escape. The horse shakes its bells (reality check)
  • Question type seen: "How does the rider describe the woods?" / "Discuss the theme"
  • Examiner's magic words: duty vs temptation, symbolism of dark woods, repetition, rural New England, death symbolism

Phase 5 — Marks Engineering & Safety Net

The Universal Answer Formula — Never Leave a Question Blank

Every poetry question — no matter how unfamiliar — can farm partial marks with this exact 3-part structure:

1
THE SETUP — Identify the poem, author, and core theme in 2–3 sentences.
Guaranteed: 2–3 marks
"This poem, [Title], was written by [Author]. It is a poem that deals with the theme of [theme: mortality / transience / love / duty]. The poet uses [imagery / irony / dramatic monologue] to convey his central message."
2
THE ATTEMPT — Explain what you actually know. Quote if possible. Even one relevant detail is worth marks.
Potential: 3–5 marks
"In the poem, the poet explores [specific point]. For example, [specific scene or image]. The line '[quote]' illustrates this beautifully, as it shows [explanation]. The poet's tone throughout is [describe tone]."
3
THE CONCLUSION — A closing statement linking back to the question. Critical judgment if possible.
Extra: 1–2 marks
"Thus, [Author] successfully conveys [theme] through [technique]. The poem remains relevant because [reason]. In answering the question, we can conclude that [direct answer to question asked]."

Section A — Grammar Safety Net

Grammar is fill-in-the-blank. You either know it or you don't. But these rules give you partial marks even when unsure:

  • If you see "ability" → can / be able to. If talking about past ability → could / was able to
  • If you see "deduction from evidence" → must (positive) or can't (negative). Never "mustn't" for deduction
  • Get + past participle = passive experience. "The bicycle got stolen." / "I don't want them to get damaged."
  • Word order rule: Subject + Auxiliary + Object + Manner + Place + Time. Auxiliaries (had, have, have been) come SECOND.
  • Relative clauses: people → who/whom/whose. Things → which/that. Place → where. Never use "which" for people.
  • Unless = "if...not". "Unless it rains" = "if it doesn't rain." Never combine "unless" with negative.

Section D — Writing Safety Net

Marks in writing tasks come from format correctness + language fluency. You have the fluency. Just nail the format:

  • Business letter: ALWAYS include the full address format + date + subject line. Missing these = instant mark deduction
  • Email: ALWAYS have a clear Subject line. Without it, the email format is incorrect
  • Paragraph: Never start with "I think..." — start with a clear topic statement. "Education is the foundation of a civilized society."
  • Formal register: avoid contractions (don't → do not), avoid casual words, use "I am writing with reference to..." not "hey"
  • Length: aim for 150–200 words. More than format correctness, markers want to see that you can write a coherent, structured piece

The Quote Hierarchy — How to Elevate Any Poetry Answer

B-grade answer (no quotes):

"The Emperor of Ice Cream is about accepting death. Stevens uses two stanzas to show life and death. The poem is about reality."

A-grade answer (with quotes):

"Stevens commands us to 'Let be be finale of seem' — a philosophical statement that reality must triumph over illusion. The repeated refrain 'The only emperor is the emperor of ice-cream' anchors the poem's central acceptance of mortality's reign over all earthly pleasures."
